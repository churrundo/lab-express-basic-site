About Thomas Sankara

Thomas Isidore Noël Sankara (21 December 1949 - 15 October 1987) was a Burkinabé military officer, Marxist revolutionary, and Pan-Africanist. He served as the President of Burkina Faso from 1983 to his assassination in 1987. Sankara was seen by his supporters as a charismatic and iconic figure of revolution【9†source】.

After being appointed Prime Minister in 1983, Sankara was eventually imprisoned due to disputes with the sitting government. While under house arrest, a group of revolutionaries seized power on his behalf in a popularly-supported coup. At the age of 33, Sankara became the President of the Republic of Upper Volta. He initiated social, ecological, and economic programs and renamed the country from Upper Volta to Burkina Faso, which translates to 'Land of Incorruptible People'. The people of the nation are known as Burkinabé, meaning 'upright people'【10†source】.

Sankara's foreign policies were centered on anti-imperialism. He rejected aid from organizations such as the International Monetary Fund and instead welcomed aid from other sources. He aimed to reduce reliance on aid by boosting domestic revenues and diversifying the sources of assistance【11†source】.

His domestic policies encompassed famine prevention, agrarian self-sufficiency, land reform, and the suspension of rural poll taxes. Sankara focused on a nationwide literacy campaign and a vaccination program against meningitis, yellow fever, and measles. His government also concentrated on building schools, health centers, water reservoirs, and infrastructure projects. He combated the desertification of the Sahel by planting over 10 million trees【12†source】.

Moreover, he outlawed female genital mutilation, forced marriages, and polygamy. Sankara set up Cuban-inspired Committees for the Defense of the Revolution. He established Popular Revolutionary Tribunals to prosecute public officials charged with political crimes and corruption, considering such elements of the state as counter-revolutionaries【13†source】.

Sankara’s revolutionary programs for African self-reliance made him an icon to many of Africa's poor. Sankara remained popular with a considerable majority of his country's citizens. However, some of his policies alienated elements of the former ruling class, including tribal leaders, and the governments of France and its ally, the Ivory Coast. On 15 October 1987, Sankara was assassinated by troops led by Blaise Compaoré, who assumed leadership of the state shortly thereafter and retained it until the 2014 Burkina Faso uprising【14†source】.
